## 1. Mathematical & Structural Sanity Check

## 2. Classification Metrics Logical Consistency

The evaluation metrics generated in `classification_stats.json` are **100% logically consistent** with your confusion matrix. Let us break down the underlying equations to verify the precision, recall, and F1 scores using the values from your matrix:

$$\text{Confusion Matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \text{TN} & \text{FP} \\ \text{FN} & \text{TP} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 42 & 1 \\ 15 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Metric Validations

* **Accuracy:**

$$\text{Accuracy} = \frac{\text{TP} + \text{TN}}{\text{Total}} = \frac{2 + 42}{60} = \frac{44}{60} \approx 0.7333 \implies \mathbf{0.73}$$


* **Class 0 (Majority Class) Metrics:**

$$\text{Precision}_0 = \frac{\text{TN}}{\text{TN} + \text{FN}} = \frac{42}{42 + 15} = \frac{42}{57} \approx 0.7368 \implies \mathbf{0.74}$$


$$\text{Recall}_0 = \frac{\text{TN}}{\text{TN} + \text{FP}} = \frac{42}{42 + 1} = \frac{42}{43} \approx 0.9767 \implies \mathbf{0.98}$$


$$\text{F1}_0 = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision}_0 \times \text{Recall}_0}{\text{Precision}_0 + \text{Recall}_0} = 2 \times \frac{0.7368 \times 0.9767}{0.7368 + 0.9767} \approx \mathbf{0.84}$$


* **Class 1 (Minority Class) Metrics:**

$$\text{Precision}_1 = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}} = \frac{2}{2 + 1} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.6666 \implies \mathbf{0.67}$$

## 3. Critical Red Flags & Architectural Quirks

While the math checks out completely, there are **two critical warnings** and **one major red flag** regarding your architecture that must be addressed before running the real 20,000-row dataset.

### 🚨 CRITICAL RED FLAG: Data Leakage in Feature Selection

Your `feature_selection.py` script reads the *entire* `normalized_csv`, computes the global Spearman correlation matrix, runs the QUBO solver, and **then** cuts the dataset into train and test splits.

> **Why this is fatal:** Your QUBO solver selected these 28 features based on their relationship with the target column across all 200 rows—including the 60 rows reserved for testing. This is a classic form of **Data Leakage**. Your test set metrics are compromised and overly optimistic because the model is evaluating features that were chosen using test-set insights.

### ⚠️ WARNING 1: The Alpha Explosion ($\alpha = 0.9727$)

Notice how your tuned $\alpha$ is sitting at `0.9727`. This means your optimization problem is allocating 97.27% of its weight to feature *relevance* (linear terms) and a tiny 2.73% to feature *redundancy* (quadratic terms).

This happens because of a combinatorial imbalance in your QUBO formulation:

* There are only 143 linear terms on the diagonal.
* There are $\frac{143 \times 142}{2} = 10,153$ off-diagonal quadratic interaction terms.

Because you have over 10,000 cross-correlation terms, even a tiny weight of $(1 - \alpha)$ generates a massive total penalty that suppresses feature selection. To get close to your target $K$, the binary search is forced to push $\alpha$ almost to 1. On your 20,000-row dataset, this effect will distort your feature selection unless you scale your off-diagonal terms by dividing them by the number of features.

### ⚠️ WARNING 2: Target Distribution Drift

Your sequential "clean cut" split resulted in class imbalances between your sets:

* **Training Set:** 20.0% ones.
* **Test Set:** 28.33% ones (17 out of 60).

On dummy random data, this is acceptable variance. However, on real financial or loan data, a sequential clean cut can introduce major sampling bias if the data has structural or temporal patterns (e.g., earlier rows represent older loans with lower default rates than newer loans).

## 4. Recommended Action Plan

To ensure your framework is production-ready, apply these modifications to your execution script:

1. **Invert the Sequence:** Modify your master execution order so that the dataset is split into training and testing sets *immediately after preprocessing*.
2. **Isolate Feature Selection:** Pass **only** the training partition to `select_features()`. Once the QUBO identifies the best 28 features, filter the test dataset down to those exact 28 features without recalculating a new correlation matrix.
3. **Normalize the Quadratic Penalty:** Adjust your QUBO objective function so that the off-diagonal matrix terms are scaled down by the total feature count ($n = 143$). This keeps $\alpha$ stable near $0.5$ instead of blowing up to $0.99$.

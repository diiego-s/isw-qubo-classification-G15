Notice how your tuned $\alpha$ is sitting at `0.9727`. This means your optimization problem is allocating 97.27% of its weight to feature *relevance* (linear terms) and a tiny 2.73% to feature *redundancy* (quadratic terms).

This happens because of a combinatorial imbalance in your QUBO formulation:

* There are only 143 linear terms on the diagonal.
* There are $\frac{143 \times 142}{2} = 10,153$ off-diagonal quadratic interaction terms.

Because you have over 10,000 cross-correlation terms, even a tiny weight of $(1 - \alpha)$ generates a massive total penalty that suppresses feature selection. To get close to your target $K$, the binary search is forced to push $\alpha$ almost to 1. On your 20,000-row dataset, this effect will distort your feature selection unless you scale your off-diagonal terms by dividing them by the number of features.

### ⚠️ WARNING 2: Target Distribution Drift

Your sequential "clean cut" split resulted in class imbalances between your sets:

* **Training Set:** 20.0% ones.
* **Test Set:** 28.33% ones (17 out of 60).

On dummy random data, this is acceptable variance. However, on real financial or loan data, a sequential clean cut can introduce major sampling bias if the data has structural or temporal patterns (e.g., earlier rows represent older loans with lower default rates than newer loans).
